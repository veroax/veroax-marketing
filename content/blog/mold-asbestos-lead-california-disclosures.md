California has a lot of old housing stock. The 1920s bungalows in Berkeley and Pasadena, the 1940s tract homes in the East Bay, the 1950s ranch houses across the San Fernando Valley, and the 1960s and 70s subdivisions throughout the state. Most of this housing was built before mold remediation was a building science, before asbestos was banned, and before lead-based paint was prohibited. When you represent buyers on these properties, you need to know what sellers must disclose, what they typically do not, and what your job is in the gap.

## Mold

Mold disclosure in California is governed by the Toxic Mold Protection Act of 2001 (Health and Safety Code section 26100 et seq.), but the practical rule for resale is narrower than agents sometimes assume.

**When must the seller disclose mold?**

The seller must disclose mold conditions of which the seller has actual knowledge. This means:

- The seller had a mold test done and has the report
- The seller had a remediation performed and has the records
- The seller observed visible mold and is aware of it
- The seller was informed by a tenant, neighbor, or contractor of a mold condition

The seller is not required to test for mold proactively. The seller is not required to disclose conditions they do not know about. A seller who has never had mold tested and has not observed visible mold can truthfully answer "no" on the disclosure and still have mold behind a wall.

**What an agent does:**

- Treat any disclosed prior water intrusion as a mold question. Water plus drywall plus time produces mold roughly 50% of the time without intervention.
- If the inspection finds elevated moisture readings or visible staining, recommend a mold air quality test. These run $300 to $600 and produce a defensible record.
- If the buyer has respiratory sensitivities or immunocompromised family members, recommend testing regardless of disclosed history.
- Document the seller's response to follow-up questions in writing.

## Asbestos

Asbestos was widely used in California residential construction through the 1970s and was not effectively banned in most building products until the early 1980s. Common locations:

- Popcorn ceilings, particularly textured ceilings in homes built before 1980
- Vinyl floor tiles and the mastic adhesive underneath them
- Pipe insulation, particularly on hot water and steam lines in older homes
- Cement asbestos siding and roofing
- Some drywall joint compound and texture products

**When must the seller disclose asbestos?**

Same rule as mold: actual knowledge. A seller who has tested for asbestos and has results must disclose. A seller who has never tested can truthfully say they have no actual knowledge. The TDS and SPQ both ask the question.

**The pre-1980 rule:**

If a home was built before 1980, assume asbestos-containing materials are present somewhere in the structure unless testing has ruled them out. This is not paranoia, it is a working rule from environmental hygienists. Popcorn ceilings, vinyl floors, and pipe wrap in a pre-1980 home are presumed asbestos until tested.

**What an agent does:**

- For pre-1980 homes, advise the buyer that asbestos is presumed present in certain materials
- Advise that asbestos in good, undisturbed condition is generally not a health risk
- Advise that any renovation, demolition, or disturbance requires proper testing and abatement
- Note that abatement is regulated by Cal/OSHA and South Coast AQMD (or the local AQMD), and requires licensed contractors
- Budget for abatement if the buyer plans renovations: popcorn ceiling removal in a 1,500 sq ft home, $2,000 to $5,000; vinyl floor abatement, $3 to $10 per sq ft; pipe insulation abatement, $200 to $500 per linear foot

## Lead-based paint

Lead-based paint is the one disclosure in this group that has federal teeth. The Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act (Title X) requires sellers of pre-1978 homes to:

1. Provide the buyer with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family from Lead in Your Home"
2. Disclose known lead-based paint hazards
3. Provide any records or reports of lead-based paint testing
4. Allow the buyer a 10-day opportunity (or other mutually agreed period) to conduct a lead inspection
5. Use the federally mandated Lead-Based Paint Disclosure form

Both agents must also sign the form, confirming they informed the parties of their obligations under the law. Penalties for failure to comply are real, up to three times the buyer's damages.

**The pre-1978 rule:**

Same logic as the pre-1980 asbestos rule, but with a federal requirement attached. Pre-1978 homes are presumed to contain lead-based paint somewhere unless testing has ruled it out. Older paint layers under newer paint are common.

**What an agent does:**

- Confirm the federal Lead-Based Paint Disclosure is in the package and signed by all parties
- Advise the buyer of their 10-day inspection right
- For families with children under 6, strongly encourage a lead inspection
- For renovation plans, advise that EPA's Renovation, Repair, and Painting (RRP) rule requires certified contractors for any disturbance of lead paint in pre-1978 housing

## When none of these are disclosed but the home is pre-1980

A common situation: pre-1980 home, no mold history disclosed, no asbestos history disclosed, no lead-based paint testing done. What do you tell the buyer?

A clean conversation looks like this:

1. "The seller has disclosed no actual knowledge of mold, asbestos, or lead. They are not required to test proactively."
2. "Given the home's age, the working assumption is that asbestos-containing materials may be present in the popcorn ceiling, vinyl flooring, and pipe insulation. Federal law presumes lead-based paint."
3. "We can order testing during the inspection contingency. Asbestos tests run $200 to $400 for a small sample set. Lead testing similar. Mold testing $300 to $600."
4. "If you plan to renovate, factor abatement into your budget. If you plan to live in the home as-is and not disturb these materials, the risk is generally manageable but not zero."

That conversation is what informed consent looks like. The buyer makes the decision, you have documented the advice.

## AB 38 reminder

For homes in High and Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zones, AB 38 also requires the seller to disclose specified fire-hardening features. This is not directly a mold/asbestos/lead issue, but it is in the same category of statutory disclosure obligations that often go unaddressed. Confirm AB 38 documentation is in the package on any fire zone property.

## Action checklist

- For pre-1978 homes: confirm federal Lead-Based Paint Disclosure is signed
- For pre-1980 homes: discuss asbestos presumption with the buyer
- For any water intrusion history: recommend mold air quality testing
- Document seller's response to follow-up questions in writing
- Budget for abatement if the buyer plans renovation work

## Bottom line

Mold, asbestos, and lead are all "actual knowledge" disclosures except for the federal lead form, which is mandatory on pre-1978 housing. In older homes, the absence of disclosure is not the absence of risk. Have the conversation, document the advice, and let the buyer make an informed choice.
