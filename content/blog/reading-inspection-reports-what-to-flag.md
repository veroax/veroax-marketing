A home inspection report is not a punch list. It is a record of observations. The inspector's job is to tell you what they saw and recommend further evaluation where appropriate. The inspector's job is not to tell you which findings matter, which are urgent, and which justify a credit. That is the agent's job, and most agents do it poorly because they read the report the way a buyer reads it: cover to cover, looking for the scary parts.

Here is how to read an inspection report the way an experienced buyer's agent reads it.

## Inspectors write neutral observations on purpose

A general home inspector's standard of care, under the California Real Estate Inspection Association (CREIA) and ASHI guidelines, is to report what is visible and accessible. They do not open walls. They do not test for capacity. They do not guess at cost. When an inspector writes "visible water staining at southwest corner of kitchen ceiling, recommend further evaluation," that is the inspector doing their job correctly. It is also a finding that, untranslated, is meaningless to a buyer.

Three reasons inspectors write neutrally:

1. **Liability.** An inspector who writes "roof is shot, replace immediately" gets sued when the roof lasts another seven years.
2. **Standards of practice.** CREIA standards prohibit inspectors from estimating repair costs or recommending specific contractors.
3. **Scope.** A general inspector is not a roofer, not an electrician, not a structural engineer. They flag for further evaluation precisely because they cannot diagnose definitively.

The translation step is the agent's job. Here are three real examples of how that translation looks.

## Example 1: Water intrusion

**Inspector wrote:** "Visible water staining at southwest corner of kitchen ceiling, approximately 18 inches in diameter. Source not determined. Recommend further evaluation by a qualified roofing contractor."

**What an agent does with this:**

- Read the staining as evidence of past or present roof leak directly above the location
- Check the roof report for findings at that corner of the home
- Check the attic inspection (if accessible) for evidence of decking damage, mold, or staining
- Note the home's roof age from the seller disclosure
- Estimate the scope: a single area repair with no decking damage is $400 to $1,200. Replacing the affected section with decking work is $3,000 to $8,000. A full roof replacement, if the inspector is flagging multiple areas, is $15,000 to $40,000 depending on size and pitch.

**Negotiation posture:** Request a roofing contractor's evaluation at seller's cost before contingency removal. If the contractor's bid is below $2K, request a credit. If it is above $5K, request a credit and a repair credit for interior drywall and paint at the staining.

## Example 2: Federal Pacific Electric (FPE) panels

**Inspector wrote:** "Main electrical panel is a Federal Pacific Electric Stab-Lok panel. These panels have been the subject of reports of failure to trip under overload conditions. Recommend evaluation by a licensed electrician."

**What an agent does with this:**

- Recognize this as a known fire safety issue, well-documented in the trade for decades
- Understand that the inspector is being polite. FPE panels are widely considered defective.
- Confirm the brand and Stab-Lok bus configuration from the inspector's photos
- Plan for replacement, not repair. Patching FPE panels does not solve the problem.
- Estimate the scope: a 200-amp panel replacement in California, including permit, $2,800 to $5,500. Higher in coastal cities with longer permit timelines.
- Note the insurance impact. Several California carriers will not write a policy on an FPE panel.

**Negotiation posture:** Request a credit equal to a licensed electrician's bid for full panel replacement, not "evaluation." Get the bid in writing before contingency removal. This is a High severity item by any reasonable standard.

## Example 3: Original galvanized supply lines

**Inspector wrote:** "Water supply lines throughout home appear to be galvanized steel. Galvanized supply lines have a finite useful life and may experience reduced flow and corrosion over time. Recommend evaluation by a licensed plumber."

**What an agent does with this:**

- Check the construction year. Galvanized was standard until roughly 1960. After that, copper became common.
- Look at the inspector's photos for flow tests at multiple fixtures. Pressure drops when a second fixture is opened are a sign of corrosion narrowing the lines.
- Check under sinks for visible corrosion at the supply stops
- Check the water heater area for the main supply line condition
- Estimate the scope: full repipe in copper or PEX, $8,000 to $20,000 for a typical 1,500 to 2,500 square foot home, depending on access (slab versus crawl, single story versus two story).

**Negotiation posture:** This is rarely a deal-killer but it is a real credit. Galvanized supply lines have an end of life, and the buyer is inheriting an aging system. Request a credit calibrated to a plumber's bid, or in some cases request seller-paid repipe before close.

## The framework

For every meaningful finding in an inspection report, run this sequence:

1. **What did the inspector actually observe?** Read the literal words, not your interpretation.
2. **What is the most likely cause?** Use your experience and cross-reference with other findings.
3. **What is the scope of the worst reasonable case?** Not the cheap fix, the realistic one.
4. **What is the dollar range?** From contractor bids, not from your guess.
5. **What is the negotiation ask?** Tied to a specific finding and a specific dollar amount.

Vague asks lose. Specific asks tied to specific findings win.

## Findings that need a specialist before negotiating

Some general inspector findings should not be negotiated until a specialist has looked at them. Examples:

- Foundation movement: get a structural engineer
- Significant electrical issues: get a licensed electrician
- Sewer lateral suspicion: get a sewer camera scope
- Roof at end of life: get a roofing contractor
- HVAC system failures: get an HVAC contractor

Doing the negotiation off the general inspector's report alone gives the seller room to push back with "your inspector isn't qualified to say that." Getting the specialist's report eliminates that move.

## Action checklist

- Read every report in the package, not just the general inspection summary
- For each meaningful finding, write down: observation, likely cause, scope, dollar range, negotiation ask
- Get specialist reports for anything outside the general inspector's expertise
- Tie every credit request to a specific finding and a specific bid
- Document everything before contingency removal

## Bottom line

Inspectors observe. Agents translate. The translation step is where deals get made or lost, and the way to do it well is to treat every finding as a question that ends in a contractor's bid, not in an opinion.
