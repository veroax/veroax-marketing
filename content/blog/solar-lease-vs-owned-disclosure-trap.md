If you have closed even a handful of transactions in California in the last five years, you have hit a solar issue. Solar is the single most common disclosure trap in modern California resale, and the deals that die from it usually die in the last week before close, when title surfaces a UCC-1 fixture filing nobody knew about, or escrow gets a payoff demand from the solar company for $34,000 that the buyer was not expecting.

Here is the landscape and the four most common failures.

## The four flavors of solar

Solar systems on a California home come in one of four configurations:

1. **Owned outright.** Cash purchase or paid-off loan. The panels are a fixture, owned by the homeowner, and convey with the property at no additional cost. This is the cleanest scenario.
2. **Solar loan, still being paid.** The homeowner financed the purchase, often through a third-party lender (GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, etc.). The loan may be secured by a UCC-1 fixture filing. Must be paid off at close.
3. **Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) or lease.** The homeowner does not own the panels. A third party (SunRun, Sunnova, Tesla, etc.) owns them and the homeowner pays for the power generated. 20-year contracts are typical. Must be transferred to buyer, or bought out by seller.
4. **PACE financing.** Property Assessed Clean Energy. The solar (and sometimes other energy improvements) was financed through a property tax assessment, originated by HERO, Ygrene (now defunct in California), or similar programs. The lien is on the property and survives sale.

Each of these has a different disclosure profile and a different escrow path. Confusing them is how deals die.

## Owned: what to confirm

For an owned, paid-off system:

- Confirm in writing that the system is owned, not leased
- Request a copy of the installer's invoice or the loan payoff statement
- Confirm there is no active UCC-1 fixture filing on the panels (escrow and title will catch this if it exists)
- Confirm warranty status: panels typically 25 years, inverter 10 to 12 years, workmanship 10 years
- Confirm any monitoring service is transferable or cancellable

Owned solar is a positive feature and should be disclosed accurately. Some agents undersell owned solar because they confuse it with a lease.

## Solar loan: payoff required

A solar loan is just a loan. At close, escrow pays it off, the lender releases the UCC-1, and title clears. The complications are:

- **Payoff amount versus value of the system.** Sellers sometimes owe more than the system is worth, particularly on early-stage loans with rolled-in fees. This is a seller problem, not a buyer problem, but it can stall closing if the seller resists.
- **UCC-1 not filed yet, or filed under the wrong name.** Document mismatches with the installer cause delays.
- **Loan structured as a lease for tax purposes.** Some "loans" are functionally PPAs in disguise. Read the document, not the salesperson's description.

## PPA and lease: the most common landmine

This is where deals fail most often. PPAs and leases are 20-year contracts. They are not paid off at close. They are either:

- **Assumed by the buyer.** The buyer signs a transfer agreement with the solar company, agreeing to take over the contract. The solar company qualifies the buyer (credit check, sometimes income verification) and approves the transfer.
- **Bought out by the seller.** The seller pays the solar company the buyout amount, which is contractually defined in the lease. Buyouts commonly run $20,000 to $40,000 on a residential system, depending on age and configuration.

Practical problems:

- **The buyer's lender may not approve the assumed payment.** PPAs are debt-like obligations and some underwriters count them against the buyer's debt-to-income ratio.
- **The solar company is slow to process the transfer.** 30 to 60 days is common. If the close-of-escrow date is 30 days out, you are already behind.
- **The buyer does not qualify.** A cash buyer with no W-2 income may fail the solar company's credit check.
- **The buyout amount has not been requested.** Sellers sometimes assume the buyer will assume the lease and do not request a buyout quote until late in escrow.

If the disclosure package mentions solar and does not specify ownership versus PPA versus lease, that is the agent's first follow-up question. Get it in writing on day one.

## PACE liens: tax assessment that survives the sale

PACE programs financed solar (and HVAC, windows, roofs) by adding a property tax assessment. The assessment runs 5 to 25 years and stays with the property unless paid off at close.

Key issues:

- **Most conventional and FHA/VA lenders will not lend on a property with an active PACE lien.** They require payoff at close.
- **The seller may not realize the PACE assessment is a lien.** Some sellers think of it as just a higher tax bill.
- **Payoff demands take time.** Some PACE administrators take 10 to 20 business days to issue a payoff statement.
- **PACE was prohibited from new origination in California in residential settings under AB 1284 and subsequent legislation, but existing assessments are still in effect on many properties.**

If you see PACE on title or in the disclosure, request the payoff demand immediately. Do not wait for escrow to chase it.

## UCC-1 fixture filings

A UCC-1 fixture filing is a security interest filed in the county records on a fixture attached to real property. Solar lenders and PPA providers commonly file UCC-1s on their panels. Title will pick these up in the preliminary title report.

For an owned-and-paid-off system, a UCC-1 that was not released at payoff is a clerical problem. Escrow contacts the original lender for a release. This can take 30 to 60 days if the original lender is unresponsive or out of business.

For a PPA or lease, the UCC-1 reflects the solar company's interest. The buyer is assuming the obligation, but the UCC-1 generally must be re-filed in the buyer's name as part of the transfer.

A UCC-1 that title cannot clear is a deal killer. Surface it on day one.

## The four most common solar disclosure failures

In our experience, these four account for the majority of solar-related deal failures:

1. **The seller answers "yes, solar is present" without specifying ownership.** Buyer assumes owned, finds out at week three of escrow it is a PPA with a $32,000 buyout.
2. **The PPA transfer is requested too late.** Close-of-escrow date is 21 days out, the solar company's transfer timeline is 30 to 45 days, the closing is delayed.
3. **PACE lien not disclosed.** Seller did not realize the additional line on the tax bill was a lien. Buyer's lender finds it in title and declines to fund.
4. **UCC-1 not released after old loan payoff.** Title cannot clear, closing is delayed weeks while escrow chases a release from a lender that has been acquired three times.

## Action checklist

- On every property with solar, ask in writing: owned, financed, PPA/lease, or PACE
- Request documentation supporting the answer
- For PPA/lease, request the assumption packet or buyout quote on day one
- For PACE, request the payoff demand on day one
- For financed solar, request the payoff statement and confirm UCC-1 release process
- Review the preliminary title report for any UCC-1 fixture filings
- Confirm with the buyer's lender how the solar obligation affects DTI

## Bottom line

Solar is not a binary "has solar / does not have solar" item. It is a four-way configuration question with a different escrow workflow for each path. Ask the configuration question on day one, get documentation in writing, and never assume "the panels look new so it must be a lease" or vice versa. The cost of getting this wrong is measured in weeks of delay and tens of thousands of dollars.
