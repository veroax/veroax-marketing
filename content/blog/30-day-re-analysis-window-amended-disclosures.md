A disclosure package is not a static document. Once a buyer is in contract, the seller has a continuing duty to disclose newly discovered material facts. That duty triggers TDS amendments more often than most agents realize, and the analysis you ran on day one of escrow can be obsolete by day ten.

This post walks through what happens when a disclosure is amended, how the buyer's leverage shifts, and why we offer a 30-day re-analysis window at no charge after the initial Veroax report.

## When do disclosures actually get amended?

In California practice, amendments are common on the TDS and uncommon on the SPQ. The TDS is statutory, the SPQ is voluntary in form but contractually required in almost every CAR-form transaction, and sellers tend to treat the SPQ as a one-time exercise. The TDS, by contrast, gets amended when:

- The seller discovers a new defect between contract and close (a leak that becomes visible after a storm)
- An inspector's report surfaces something the seller had previously assumed was minor
- A neighbor or contractor mentions something the seller should have disclosed and didn't
- Permit research reveals an unpermitted addition the seller had forgotten about

Civil Code section 1102.3 gives the buyer a three-day right of rescission after delivery of an amended TDS by personal delivery, or five days after delivery by mail. That rescission right resets every time a material amendment lands. Most buyers and many agents do not realize this.

## A worked example

Listing comes on the market in Walnut Creek, a 1972 single-story home, 1,850 square feet. Original TDS is signed by the seller with the kitchen plumbing section marked "no known defects." Buyer's inspector flags a small efflorescence stain in the kitchen sink base cabinet but does not call out an active leak. Buyer goes into contract at $1.275M.

Five days into escrow, the seller emails the listing agent. They had a "very slow drip" under the kitchen sink that they fixed themselves with plumber's putty a few months back. Listing agent prepares an amended TDS noting "previous slow drip at kitchen sink supply line, sealed by seller."

What just changed?

1. **The buyer's three-day rescission right resets.** They can walk on the disclosure alone.
2. **The inspection finding goes from cosmetic to confirmed.** The efflorescence stain in the cabinet is now corroborated by a seller-disclosed leak.
3. **The repair scope expands.** A DIY plumber's putty fix on a supply line is not a fix. The buyer's leverage is now a real plumbing diagnostic and likely a supply line replacement, $300 to $800 if it is one fitting, more if the line itself is galvanized.
4. **The cabinet itself may be compromised.** Particleboard cabinet bases that have been wet are structurally weakened. That is a separate cost.

Without the amendment, the buyer had a stain in a cabinet and no leverage. With the amendment, the buyer has a documented seller-disclosed leak, a corroborating inspection finding, and a fresh rescission window.

## Why the analysis has to be re-run

If you generated a disclosure analysis on day one of escrow, that analysis flagged the cabinet stain as a Moderate item and probably recommended a plumbing follow-up. The amended TDS upgrades that finding to High and shifts the negotiating posture. A static report does not capture that. A re-run does.

This is where the 30-day re-analysis window matters. Most disclosure tools (and most agents working from a yellow legal pad) treat the package as a one-time review. We treat it as a living document during escrow. If the seller amends, the buyer's agent uploads the amendment, and the analysis updates with:

- The new finding flagged
- Severity recalibrated where appropriate
- Cross-references to existing inspection findings that now read differently
- Updated negotiation talking points

Thirty days is the typical span of an escrow plus a buffer. If you need a re-analysis on day 35, you will get one, but in our experience 95% of amendments land in the first three weeks after acceptance.

## What an agent should do when an amendment arrives

A clean workflow when the listing agent sends an amended TDS or SPQ:

1. **Acknowledge receipt in writing and date-stamp it.** This starts the rescission clock cleanly.
2. **Read the amendment side-by-side with the original.** Pull out a redline if the differences are not obvious.
3. **Re-run the analysis.** Either through Veroax or by walking your inspection findings back through the new disclosure.
4. **Brief the buyer the same day.** The rescission clock is short. The buyer needs the new information immediately.
5. **Decide on a path: rescind, renegotiate, or accept.** Document the decision and the rationale.

Do not let an amended disclosure sit in your inbox for 48 hours. The buyer's clock is running.

## Common amendment pitfalls

A few traps that show up regularly:

- **The amendment is delivered informally.** The listing agent sends a text, not a signed TDS amendment. The clock does not start until the amendment is delivered in the form the statute requires. Insist on the signed form.
- **The amendment is "buried" in a supplemental disclosure.** A new item shows up in a contractor's invoice attached to a transmittal email. That is constructive disclosure but you need to flag it explicitly with the buyer.
- **The buyer waives the rescission right unknowingly.** A buyer who proceeds to remove contingencies after receiving an amendment may have waived the rescission right. Get a written acknowledgment from the buyer that they reviewed the amendment before contingency removal.
