The Transfer Disclosure Statement is the single most important document in a California residential resale, and it is also the document agents are most likely to skim. Civil Code section 1102 et seq. requires it on virtually every one-to-four unit residential sale (with narrow exemptions for probate, trustee sales, and a few other transfers). If you represent buyers in California, you will read hundreds of TDS forms in your career. Read this one carefully.

## What the TDS actually is

The TDS is the seller's statutory disclosure of known material facts about the property's condition. It is a disclosure, not a warranty. The seller is not promising the roof works. The seller is stating whether, to the best of the seller's knowledge, there have been problems with the roof. That distinction matters when something fails after closing.

The form is structured in three sections:

- **Section I** is completed by the seller. This is the heart of the form.
- **Section II** is the listing agent's visual inspection disclosure.
- **Section III** is the buyer's agent's visual inspection disclosure.

Sections II and III are not optional. Both agents owe an independent visual inspection duty under Civil Code 2079, and the TDS is where that inspection is memorialized.

## Section I, the seller's disclosure

Section I starts with a checklist of items present at the property (range, dishwasher, pool, septic, etc.) and then asks the seller whether they are aware of any "significant defects or malfunctions" in roughly a dozen systems: interior walls, ceilings, floors, foundation, roof, plumbing, electrical, and so on.

Things to look for when you read Section I:

- **"No" answers across the board.** A 1962 home with no known defects in any system is statistically improbable. Either the seller is a non-occupant trustee (check the exemption box), or they are not being careful with their answers.
- **Inconsistencies with the property profile.** Solar panels on the roof but no mention of solar in the systems checklist. A pool visible in MLS photos but unchecked on the form.
- **"Explanation" attachments.** When the seller checks "yes" anywhere, there should be an attached explanation. Missing attachments are a common defect in the package.
- **Neighborhood noise, nuisance, or zoning items.** These are often left blank because the seller "got used to it." Cross-check with Google Street View and the SPQ.

## Sections II and III, the agent inspections

The agent's visual inspection is limited to "readily observable" conditions in accessible areas. You do not need to climb on the roof or open the electrical panel. You do need to walk every room, look at the ceilings, look under sinks, and note what a reasonably competent agent would see.

What I see missed most often:

- Water staining on ceilings under upstairs bathrooms
- Cracked or settled exterior stucco at corners and below windows
- Visible cracks in concrete slab at garage or patio
- Trip hazards from heaved walkways
- Evidence of DIY electrical work (mixed outlet styles, exposed Romex in garage)

Write what you saw, not your interpretation. "Water staining approximately 12 inches in diameter at master bedroom ceiling, southwest corner" is a defensible entry. "Old water damage, repaired" is a guess that can come back at you.

## Reconciling the TDS with inspection reports

The general inspection, roof report, pest (Section I / II) report, and any specialist reports will frequently contradict the TDS. The seller said "no" to roof problems, and the roof inspector flags three areas of cracked flashing and a 15-year asphalt roof at year 18. What now?

A few practical rules:

1. **The inspection report governs current condition.** The TDS speaks to seller knowledge.
2. **Document the gap in writing.** A request for repair or credit should cite the inspection report finding, not characterize the seller as dishonest.
3. **Ambiguity is leverage, not evidence.** "Unknown" on a TDS line is not an admission. It is a starting point for diligence.
4. **When in doubt, get a specialist.** A general inspector flagging "possible foundation settlement, recommend evaluation" without a structural engineer's follow-up is half a finding.

## Disclosure is not warranty

This is the line every buyer needs to hear from their agent at least once. If the seller did not know the water heater was failing, and disclosed accordingly, and it fails three weeks after close, the buyer's recourse is limited. The TDS is a snapshot of seller knowledge on the date signed. It is not an insurance policy.

Where buyers do have recourse is when the seller knew and did not disclose. Proving that after the fact is hard. Prevent it by reading the form carefully on the front end, asking pointed follow-up questions in writing, and getting independent inspections that you can rely on.

## Action checklist

Before you sign off on the TDS for your buyer, run through this:

- Confirm Section I is signed and dated by every seller on title
- Cross-reference Section I yes-answers against attached explanations
- Verify the listing agent's Section II is signed and dated, not blank
- Complete your Section III with specific, observable notes from your walkthrough
- Compare every "no" in Section I against the general inspection summary
- Flag any inconsistency in writing and request clarification before contingency removal
- Save a clean PDF of the executed TDS in your transaction file

## Bottom line

The TDS is a disclosure, not a warranty, and the agent's job is to read it like a deposition exhibit. Match every answer against what the inspections show, document the gaps, and never let a buyer remove the inspection contingency on a TDS you have not personally reconciled with the inspection scope.
