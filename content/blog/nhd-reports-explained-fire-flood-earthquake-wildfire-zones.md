The Natural Hazard Disclosure report is the document most agents skim and most buyers do not understand. It is also, after the recent CALFIRE wildfire zone updates, the single fastest-changing piece of the disclosure package. If you sold a home in a foothill community two years ago, the same address may now be in a Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone that did not exist on the map then.

Here is what is in an NHD, what changed in the last two years, and what to do with the information.

## The six statutory hazard zones

California Civil Code section 1103 requires the seller to disclose whether the property is in any of six statutory natural hazard zones:

1. **Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA).** Federally designated by FEMA. Triggers federal flood insurance requirement on federally backed loans.
2. **Area of Potential Flooding (Dam Inundation Area).** State-designated. Areas that would flood if a specific dam failed.
3. **Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone (VHFHSZ).** Designated by CALFIRE in State Responsibility Areas and by local agencies in Local Responsibility Areas.
4. **Wildland-Urban Interface Fire Area (WUI).** Local designation for areas adjacent to wildland.
5. **Earthquake Fault Zone (Alquist-Priolo Zone).** Surface fault rupture risk. Restricts construction across known active faults.
6. **Seismic Hazard Zone.** Risk of liquefaction or earthquake-induced landslide.

Most California sellers contract with a third-party NHD provider (JCP, Disclosure Source, MyNHD, etc.) to generate the report. The provider's report is the seller's compliance with the disclosure requirement, and the provider carries E&O on the determination.

## What changed: the CALFIRE 2025 map update

CALFIRE's Fire Hazard Severity Zone maps were significantly revised in 2024 and 2025, reflecting updated fuel and weather data. The result is that many properties that were previously in Moderate or High zones are now in Very High zones, and many properties that were not on the map at all are now in High or Very High zones.

The practical implications for an agent:

- An NHD report run before mid-2025 may not reflect the updated maps
- A property near the wildland-urban interface should be re-checked against the current CALFIRE map at calfire.ca.gov before contingency removal
- The seller's NHD provider should be using the current maps. If the report is older than six months, request a refresh.

## What VHFHSZ status actually means

When a property is in a Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone, three things follow:

### 1. Insurance availability is constrained

California's admitted carriers have been pulling back from VHFHSZ properties for years. Many properties in these zones now rely on:

- The California FAIR Plan, the insurer of last resort, with limited coverage (dwelling only, no liability, capped at $3M for residential)
- A wrap policy from a surplus lines carrier for liability and contents
- Combined cost frequently 2 to 4 times what an admitted carrier would charge in a non-hazard zone

A buyer in contract on a VHFHSZ property needs to call an insurance broker on day one of escrow, not day twelve. Insurance binders take longer to issue in hazard zones, and the lender will not fund without one.

### 2. AB 38 defensible space and home hardening disclosure

Assembly Bill 38, effective 2021, requires sellers in High and Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zones to:

- Provide documentation of compliance with defensible space requirements (Public Resources Code section 4291)
- Disclose specified retrofit features of the home related to fire hardening

The defensible space inspection is the seller's responsibility, and a buyer's agent should confirm the documentation is in the package. Missing AB 38 documentation on a VHFHSZ property is a real finding.

### 3. Lender and underwriting impact

Some lenders apply additional underwriting overlays on VHFHSZ properties. Jumbo lenders sometimes require a third-party fire risk assessment. FHA and VA do not categorically exclude VHFHSZ properties but the appraiser will note the hazard.

## Flood zone notes

A Special Flood Hazard Area designation triggers a federal flood insurance requirement on any federally backed loan. The buyer needs:

- A flood elevation certificate (the seller usually has one if the home is post-FIRM)
- A flood insurance quote, which can range from $500 per year in low-risk AE zones to $4,000-plus per year in VE coastal zones
- An understanding of the difference between FEMA's published maps and the more conservative private flood models that some carriers use

The new FEMA Risk Rating 2.0 methodology, in effect since 2021 with full implementation by 2023, has changed pricing significantly. Premiums on some properties have doubled. A buyer relying on the prior owner's premium as a budget is going to be unpleasantly surprised.

## Earthquake zones in practice

Alquist-Priolo Earthquake Fault Zones restrict construction across known active faults. If a property is in an AP zone, the lender will sometimes require evidence that the existing structure is not built across the fault trace. A geotechnical or fault study, if available, should be in the disclosure package.

Seismic Hazard Zones (liquefaction and earthquake-induced landslide) do not restrict construction but do require a seller disclosure. Liquefaction risk in particular is concentrated in low-lying alluvial areas in the Bay Area and parts of LA.

California earthquake insurance is offered through the California Earthquake Authority (CEA) and is separate from homeowner's insurance. It is expensive, with high deductibles (typically 15 to 25 percent of the dwelling value). Most California homeowners do not carry it. The buyer should make an informed choice, not a default one.

## Action steps when a property is in a VHFHSZ

If your buyer is in contract on a VHFHSZ property, here is the sequence:

1. Day 1: Call an insurance broker. Get a written binder commitment before any contingency removal.
2. Day 2: Confirm the NHD report uses the current CALFIRE map.
3. Day 3: Confirm AB 38 defensible space documentation is in the package.
4. Day 5: If insurance comes back FAIR Plan only, recalculate the buyer's monthly carrying cost and confirm they can still afford the property.
5. Before contingency removal: Have insurance bound, in writing, with the correct property address and dwelling coverage.

The order matters. Inspections do not matter if the property is uninsurable.

## Action checklist

- Read the NHD report cover to cover, not just the summary box
- Confirm the report date is recent and uses current maps
- For VHFHSZ properties, get an insurance binder before contingency removal
- For SFHA properties, get a flood insurance quote and an elevation certificate
- For Alquist-Priolo zones, look for a fault study in the package
- Confirm AB 38 documentation on High and Very High Fire zones
- Discuss CEA earthquake insurance with the buyer regardless of zone

## Bottom line

The NHD is not a checkbox. Hazard zones drive insurance availability, insurance availability drives loan approval, and loan approval drives whether the deal closes. Read the report on day one, get the insurance call in motion immediately, and never let a buyer remove contingencies on a hazard property without a bound policy.
