Most disclosure-driven renegotiations in California fail not because the findings are weak, but because the agent presented them badly. The seller has read the same inspection report you have, and the seller's agent has already triaged it. A vague, overbroad, or poorly timed credit request gets dismissed in 30 seconds. A specific, well-timed, dollar-anchored request gets discussed.

Here is what works, what does not, and how to time it.

## What works

### 1. Tying a specific finding to a specific dollar amount

The strongest credit requests look like this:

"Per the roof inspection dated [date], pages 3 and 7, the roof shows multiple areas of cracked flashing and lifted shingles. The inspector recommends evaluation by a roofing contractor. We obtained a bid from [contractor name], license #, dated [date], for $8,500 to replace flashing, repair lifted shingles, and address the underlying decking exposure noted. We request a credit of $8,500 at close."

What makes this work:

- A specific document and page number
- A specific contractor and license
- A specific dollar amount
- A specific scope of work
- No emotion, no characterization

The seller's agent can verify every element. The number is defensible. The request is granular enough to negotiate around (the seller might counter at $6,500, but the conversation is real).

### 2. Requesting repair at seller's contractor's choice

For some findings, the buyer is better off requesting that the seller perform the repair, at the seller's contractor of choice (with reasonable qualifications), rather than taking a credit.

When this works:

- The repair is one the buyer does not want to manage personally
- The buyer is concerned about cost overruns
- The buyer wants the repair documented and warranted before close
- The repair scope is well-defined and verifiable

The language to use: "Seller to repair [specific scope] prior to close, work to be performed by a licensed contractor in good standing, with permits where required, and documentation of completed work delivered to buyer at least 3 days prior to close."

This limits the buyer's exposure to a bad repair (because the seller is on the hook for quality) and keeps the scope contained.

### 3. Backing out within the disclosure contingency window

Civil Code section 1102.3 gives the buyer a three-day right of rescission after personal delivery of the TDS, or five days after delivery by mail. This is separate from the inspection contingency in the CAR Residential Purchase Agreement, which is typically 17 days for inspections.

When the disclosure is materially deficient, the buyer's strongest move is sometimes to rescind rather than renegotiate. This is most effective when:

- The disclosure is materially incomplete (missing TDS, missing SPQ, missing AB 38 documentation on a fire zone property)
- An amendment introduces a major new finding (active leak, foundation issue, code violation)
- The buyer's risk tolerance is exhausted

A clean rescission notice cites the statute, identifies the deficiency, and preserves the buyer's deposit. Most rescissions are leverage rather than terminations: a credible rescission notice often produces a substantive credit offer within 24 hours.

### 4. Bundling and prioritizing the request

A credit request that lists 14 items, all valued separately, looks like a fishing expedition. A credit request that bundles items into 3 to 5 categories, each tied to a finding and a dollar amount, looks professional.

A clean bundle might be:

- Roof and flashing: $8,500 (per [contractor] bid)
- Electrical panel replacement (FPE Stab-Lok): $4,200 (per [electrician] bid)
- Sewer lateral root intrusion: $6,800 (per camera scope and [contractor] bid)
- Miscellaneous moderate items: $2,500 lump sum

Total: $22,000

That request will be discussed. A scattershot list of $850 for a water heater valve and $400 for missing GFCI outlets will not be.

## What does not work

### 1. Vague "the house has issues" requests

"The inspection turned up a lot of items and we want $20,000 off the price." This gets countered with "what items?" and the buyer has just lost the negotiation. Generic asks invite generic dismissals.

### 2. Waiting until contingency removal to renegotiate

The CAR RPA in California provides for an active contingency removal: the buyer must affirmatively remove contingencies in writing. The seller cannot force removal except through a Notice to Buyer to Perform, which gives the buyer two days (or whatever is specified) to remove or rescind.

Buyers who wait until the day before contingency removal to surface major findings lose leverage two ways:

- They look like they are stalling
- The seller's agent has had no time to evaluate and respond, which inclines them to dig in

A proper workflow surfaces findings within 7 to 10 days of receiving the disclosures and inspection reports, with a written request including dollar figures within that window. The remaining time before contingency removal is for negotiation, not initial discovery.

### 3. Demanding cosmetic repairs that should have been baked into the offer price

This is the biggest tell of an inexperienced buyer's agent. Asking the seller to repaint a bedroom, replace worn carpet, or fix a wobbly toilet seat in a credit request undermines every other item on the list.

These items should be priced into the offer, not negotiated post-disclosure. Cosmetic deferred maintenance is part of "what you see is what you get" on most resale homes.

### 4. Threatening to walk without being prepared to walk

A credible threat to terminate the contract is leverage. An empty threat is a tell. If the buyer is not actually prepared to walk, do not put the words in the credit request. Find another lever.

## Timing matters

A clean disclosure-driven renegotiation timeline in California, on a standard 30-day escrow:

- **Day 1 to 3:** Disclosures and inspections in motion
- **Day 7 to 10:** Inspection reports received, analysis run, findings triaged
- **Day 10 to 12:** Initial credit/repair request submitted to listing agent in writing
- **Day 12 to 15:** Back-and-forth negotiation, specialist bids obtained as needed
- **Day 15 to 17:** Agreement reached, addendum signed
- **Day 17:** Contingencies removed

If your buyer is at day 16 and has not started the conversation, the negotiation is going to be rushed and weak.

## When the seller refuses to negotiate

Sometimes the seller refuses any credit or repair. At that point the buyer has three choices:

1. Accept the property as disclosed and proceed
2. Walk away within the contingency window
3. Make a final, narrower request focused on the Critical and High items only

Walking away is not a failure. A buyer who closes on a property with $40,000 of un-credited issues is going to be unhappy 60 days after close, and that unhappiness lands on the agent.

## Action checklist

- Surface findings within 7 to 10 days of receiving disclosures
- Tie every credit request to a specific finding and a specific bid
- Bundle items into 3 to 5 categories, not a long line-item list
- Drop cosmetic items from the negotiation
- Use the right tool: credit for things the buyer can manage, repair-at-seller's-contractor for things the buyer cannot
- Preserve the rescission right and use it as leverage when warranted
- Have the buyer's walk-away decision made before contingency removal

## Bottom line

Disclosure findings are leverage when they are specific, dollar-anchored, and well-timed. They are noise when they are vague, late, or padded with cosmetic items. Treat the renegotiation as a professional document, not a wish list, and your credit requests will get taken seriously.
