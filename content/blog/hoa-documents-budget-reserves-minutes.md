The HOA disclosure packet on a California condo or planned unit development typically runs 60 to 200 pages. CC&Rs, bylaws, rules, budget, reserve study, last year of minutes, financial statements, insurance certificate, pending litigation disclosures, and a stack of statutory notices. Most agents flip through it and hand it to the buyer. Most buyers do not read it either.

You do not need to read all 200 pages. You need to find four things. Here they are.

## 1. Reserve study fund percentage

The reserve study is the financial backbone of an HOA. It is the actuarial estimate of the cost to repair and replace common elements (roof, paint, asphalt, pool, elevators, etc.) over their useful life, and the cash on hand to do it.

The number you want is the **percent funded** ratio. Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act and California Civil Code section 5550 require associations to update the reserve study every three years and disclose the funding status.

Benchmarks from association management practice:

- **90% or higher:** Strong. The HOA is on track. Special assessments are unlikely barring an unexpected event.
- **70 to 89%:** Adequate. Manageable, but watch the trend over the last several studies.
- **50 to 69%:** Underfunded. Special assessments are likely in the next several years.
- **Under 50%:** Red flag. Significant special assessment risk. Lender concerns. Insurance concerns.
- **Under 30%:** Deal-killer territory. Many lenders will not lend on an HOA below 30% funded.

Find this number in the reserve study summary or in the budget narrative. If you cannot find it in five minutes, the HOA management company has not done a proper job and that is itself a flag.

## 2. Special assessments in the last 12 to 24 months of minutes

Read the board meeting minutes, not the annual newsletter. Minutes are where bad news shows up first.

Look for:

- **Discussion of a pending or proposed special assessment.** Often phrased as "the board reviewed bids for the siding replacement project, estimated $1.4M, funded through a special assessment of approximately $18,000 per unit." This is the moment the assessment is real, even if not yet voted.
- **Recent special assessments that have already been levied.** These are sometimes structured as monthly installments rather than lump sums and a buyer needs to know whether they inherit the remaining installments.
- **Increases in regular dues above the statutory 20% cap.** California Civil Code section 5605 generally limits annual dues increases to 20% without a member vote. An above-cap increase requires member approval and signals financial stress.
- **Deferred maintenance discussions.** "The board agreed to defer the parking lot resurfacing for another year." Deferred maintenance becomes special assessment fuel.

If the minutes are missing for any month in the last 12, ask why. Missing minutes is sometimes innocent and sometimes hiding a contentious meeting.

## 3. Pending litigation against the association

Litigation against the HOA matters for two reasons: it can drain reserves, and it can make the property unfinanceable. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac have specific overlays on HOA litigation, particularly construction defect litigation and personal injury cases above policy limits.

Find this in:

- The statutory litigation disclosure (required under Civil Code section 4525)
- The HOA's certified financials, in the notes to financial statements
- The minutes, where settlements and legal expenses are recorded
- The estoppel certificate the title company will request

A construction defect lawsuit against the developer is generally less alarming than a personal injury lawsuit against the HOA, because the developer cases often produce settlements that benefit the HOA. Read the disclosure carefully and ask the management company for clarification if anything is ambiguous.

## 4. Pending rule changes (especially short-term rental restrictions)

This is the one most agents miss. HOAs amend rules constantly, and the rules in effect today may not be the rules in effect when your buyer closes.

Pay particular attention to:

- **Short-term rental restrictions.** Especially relevant in coastal, mountain, and vacation communities. An HOA that allowed Airbnb a year ago may have voted in a 30-day minimum since. If your buyer is buying as a short-term rental, this is a deal-killer.
- **Long-term rental caps.** Some associations cap the percentage of units that can be rented at any time. If the cap is full, your buyer cannot rent the unit even long-term.
- **Pet restrictions.** Weight limits, breed restrictions, count limits. Easy to miss until your buyer moves in with two large dogs.
- **Architectural rules.** Window replacement, exterior paint, deck modifications. Often more restrictive than buyers expect.
- **EV charger and solar rules.** California law (Civil Code 4745 and 4746) protects owner rights to install these, but HOAs vary in how they handle the architectural review.

## The 5-minute scan checklist

Open the HOA packet and find:

1. The reserve study percent funded (one number, usually in the executive summary)
2. The current monthly dues, plus any special assessment installments still being collected
3. Any mention of a pending or proposed special assessment in the last 12 to 24 months of minutes
4. The litigation disclosure (one paragraph or one form, statutory format)
5. Any rule changes voted or proposed in the last 12 months, especially rental restrictions

If all four come back clean, the HOA is healthy enough to move on. If any one of them flags, dig deeper before contingency removal.

## A few other items worth a quick look

Beyond the four primary items, glance at:

- **Insurance certificate.** Confirm the HOA's master policy covers what the buyer's lender requires (HO-6 walls-in coverage is usually the buyer's responsibility).
- **Delinquency rate.** California Civil Code section 5300 requires HOAs to disclose the delinquency rate. Over 15% is a flag. Over 25% raises lender financing concerns.
- **Owner-occupancy ratio.** Fannie/Freddie require 50% owner occupancy for most non-investor loans on condos. Below 50%, the buyer is into investor financing or cash.

## Action checklist

- Find and record the reserve study percent funded
- Scan the last 12 months of minutes for special assessment language
- Read the litigation disclosure in full
- Note any pending or recent rule changes, especially rental restrictions
- Check the delinquency rate and owner-occupancy ratio
- Confirm the master insurance policy meets lender requirements
- Brief the buyer on findings before contingency removal

## Bottom line

The HOA packet is large but the answers are concentrated in four places. Find the reserve number, read the recent minutes, check for litigation, and confirm the rules. Five minutes well spent, and your buyer does not get surprised by an $18,000 siding assessment six months after close.
