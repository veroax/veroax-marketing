Ask ten California agents what the difference is between the TDS and the SPQ and you will get four answers, three of which are wrong. The most common wrong answer is "the SPQ is the same as the TDS, just longer." That is a costly misread. The two forms serve different purposes, cover different ground, and should be read as complementary documents, not duplicates.

## What the TDS is

The Transfer Disclosure Statement is required by Civil Code section 1102 et seq. It is statutory, mandatory on virtually every one-to-four unit residential resale, and cannot be waived by contract. The form itself is short, roughly three pages, and structured into the seller's disclosure (Section I), the listing agent's visual inspection (Section II), and the buyer's agent's visual inspection (Section III).

The TDS is narrowly scoped. It asks about the property's physical systems (roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, etc.), about a handful of statutory hazards (fill soil, settling, drainage, neighborhood noise), and about known violations of building codes or zoning. It does not ask about HOA assessments, neighborhood disputes, prior insurance claims, or owner improvements without permits in the way the SPQ does.

## What the SPQ is

The Seller Property Questionnaire is a California Association of Realtors form, not a statutory form. It is voluntary in the sense that no statute mandates it, but the CAR Residential Purchase Agreement (RPA) requires it as a contractual term, and in practice it is present on essentially every transaction that uses CAR forms (which is the vast majority).

The SPQ is long, typically eight to ten pages, and it goes deeper than the TDS on:

- **Neighborhood issues.** Ongoing disputes with neighbors, recurring noise, parking problems, encroachments
- **HOA-specific matters.** Pending assessments, litigation, rule changes, architectural disputes
- **Attic, crawl space, and other accessibility.** Whether the seller has accessed these areas and what they observed
- **Owner improvements without permits.** Specific question, often the source of major surprises
- **Prior insurance claims.** Including water damage, fire, and liability claims
- **Trees, drainage, and easement issues.** Often the source of post-close disputes

The SPQ is also where sellers tend to be more candid, because the questions are more specific. A seller who answered "no known defects" on the TDS will often check "yes" on the SPQ when asked whether they installed the deck themselves without a permit.

## The myth that the SPQ is "just a longer TDS"

The SPQ and TDS overlap on the physical systems questions. That is what gives rise to the myth. But the overlap is incomplete, and the questions are framed differently. The TDS asks whether the seller is aware of "significant defects or malfunctions" in the roof. The SPQ asks whether there have been any roof repairs in the last five years, regardless of whether they constituted defects.

A seller can truthfully answer "no" on the TDS roof question and "yes" on the SPQ roof repair question. Both answers can be accurate. An agent reading only the TDS misses the fact that the roof has been repaired three times.

## Where the two forms diverge in practice

A few examples from real California transactions:

- **Unpermitted work.** The TDS asks about known code or zoning violations. The SPQ asks specifically about additions or alterations without permits. A seller who pulled no permit but did not consider it a "violation" will often answer the SPQ question accurately while leaving the TDS question blank.
- **HOA assessments.** The TDS has no specific HOA assessment question. The SPQ has multiple. A pending special assessment for siding replacement, $18K per unit, shows up on the SPQ and nowhere else in the package.
- **Prior insurance claims.** Not on the TDS. On the SPQ. A water damage claim from 2019 is critical context for an inspection finding of staining today.
- **Neighborhood disputes.** A boundary dispute with the neighbor, ongoing for three years, shows up on the SPQ and is otherwise invisible.

## How to read them together

Treat the TDS and SPQ as one combined document for analysis purposes. The right workflow:

1. Read the TDS first. Note every "yes" answer and the attached explanations.
2. Read the SPQ second, cross-referencing the same systems.
3. Flag any discrepancy. If the TDS says no roof defects and the SPQ says two roof repairs in five years, that is a follow-up question.
4. Pay particular attention to SPQ sections that have no TDS analog: HOA, neighborhood, insurance claims, owner improvements.
5. Read both alongside the general inspection summary.

A disclosure analysis that uses only the TDS will miss roughly a quarter of the material findings on a typical resale. We have seen it on hundreds of packages.

## Common SPQ traps

Things that show up on the SPQ that agents miss:

- **"Have you ever filed an insurance claim?" answered yes with no detail.** Demand the carrier name, claim number, and resolution. This affects the buyer's CLUE report and future insurance availability.
- **"Are you aware of any past or present settling, soil movement, or earth stability problems?"** This is a different question than the TDS foundation question and often catches different problems.
- **"Are you aware of any work done on the property without required permits?"** A "yes" here triggers a permit history search at the building department.
- **"Are you aware of any pending or threatened litigation?"** Including HOA litigation, which can make the property uninsurable or unfinanceable.

## Action checklist

- Pull both the TDS and SPQ before you brief the buyer
- Read them side by side, system by system
- Flag every discrepancy in writing and request clarification
- Pay extra attention to SPQ sections without a TDS analog
- Combine the two with the inspection report into a single analysis
- Do not let a buyer remove contingencies on a TDS alone

## Bottom line

The TDS is what the statute requires. The SPQ is where the truth often hides. Read them together, treat the discrepancies as questions to be answered, and never assume the longer form is just the shorter one with more paragraphs.
