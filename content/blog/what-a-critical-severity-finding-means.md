Every disclosure package contains dozens of findings, and almost none of them are equally important. A loose toilet seat and a Federal Pacific Electric panel both show up in the same report, and a buyer reading it cold cannot tell which one ends the deal. The agent's job is triage.

At Veroax we use a four-tier severity rubric to sort findings: Critical, High, Moderate, and Cosmetic. The labels are not magic. They are a forcing function that helps agents surface the right items to clients in the right order. Here is what each tier means in practice.

## Critical: stops the deal or kills the financing

A Critical finding is one of two things: a health and safety issue serious enough that a reasonable buyer would walk, or a condition that will cause the lender or insurer to decline. If you find one of these and your buyer is still in contract, the conversation is "should we proceed at all," not "how much credit do we ask for."

Concrete California examples:

- **Active foundation failure with measurable movement.** A structural engineer's report citing differential settlement of more than half an inch with active progression. Repair scope routinely runs $40K to $150K plus.
- **Property sits in a CALFIRE Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zone and the insurance carrier has issued non-renewal.** Without a written binder, the deal cannot close.
- **Active leak from a buried sewer lateral with documented backups.** Repair via trenchless lining or open cut, $8K to $25K, plus the city's point-of-sale lateral compliance ordinance in places like Oakland and Berkeley.
- **Unpermitted addition that the city has flagged for code enforcement.** Lender will not lend on square footage outside the permit record, and the city can require demolition.
- **Open PACE lien for solar or HVAC that exceeds the buyer's loan-to-value tolerance.** PACE attaches to the property and survives sale.

## High: significant negotiation leverage

A High finding is real, expensive, and worth a specific dollar credit or repair demand. The deal does not die over it, but the buyer is meaningfully worse off if they ignore it.

Concrete California examples:

- **Federal Pacific Electric (FPE) Stab-Lok panel.** Known to fail to trip under overload. Full panel replacement, $3K to $6K. Most home inspectors will flag it as a fire safety concern.
- **Galvanized steel water supply lines throughout.** Corrosion from the inside out, pressure drops, eventual pinhole leaks. Repipe in copper or PEX runs $8K to $20K depending on home size and access.
- **Roof at end of useful life.** A 25-year composition roof in year 23 with multiple repairs in the last five years. $15K to $40K depending on pitch, size, and tear-off requirements.
- **Sewer lateral with root intrusion or partial collapse on camera.** Even without active backups, the buyer is one season from a problem.
- **HOA reserve study under 30% funded with no special assessment levied.** Assessment is coming, and it is the buyer's problem if it lands after close.

## Moderate: should be addressed but will not kill the deal

A Moderate finding belongs in the inspection summary that goes to the client, but it is not a contingency-removal blocker on its own. These are the items where a smart buyer requests a credit but does not threaten to walk.

Concrete California examples:

- **GFCI outlets missing in kitchen, bathrooms, garage, or exterior.** Code requires them in wet locations. Add a few outlets, $150 to $400 total.
- **Water heater past 10 years but functional.** Plan for replacement in the next 24 months. Tank replacement runs $1,800 to $3,500 installed.
- **Single-pane aluminum windows in coastal climates.** Energy efficiency hit and corrosion potential, but not failing. Replace at homeowner's pace, $800 to $1,500 per window.
- **Bathroom fan venting into the attic rather than to exterior.** Moisture accumulation risk. Reroute, $200 to $600.
- **Minor drainage issue, downspouts terminating at the foundation.** Add splash blocks or extensions, $50 to $200.

## Cosmetic: noted, not actionable

Cosmetic findings exist in every report. The inspector lists them because the inspector lists everything. The agent's job is to keep these from cluttering the credit request and burying the High findings.

Concrete California examples:

- Scuffs on interior paint
- A single missing screen
- A toilet seat that wobbles
- Dirty HVAC filter (the seller will replace if asked, but a buyer who leads with this loses credibility)
- Worn caulking at a tub edge

If a buyer presents 14 items to the seller and 11 of them are cosmetic, the seller agent reads the request, rolls their eyes, and pushes back on the three that actually mattered. Triage protects your leverage.

## How to use this in practice

When you sit down with a disclosure package, sort the findings into these four buckets before you draft anything. Tell your buyer:

1. Here are the Critical items. We need to decide today whether you still want this house.
2. Here are the High items. These are where we ask for real money or real repairs.
3. Here are the Moderate items. We will bundle a credit request.
4. Here are the Cosmetic items. We are noting them for your benefit, not negotiating them.

That conversation, done well, takes 20 minutes and saves a deal.

## Action checklist

- Read every report in the disclosure package before you call the buyer
- Sort findings into Critical, High, Moderate, Cosmetic
- Lead the buyer call with Critical, get a "proceed or walk" decision
- Build the credit or repair request from High and Moderate only
- Keep Cosmetic items in the file for the buyer's reference, not the negotiation

## Bottom line

Severity is not a feature. It is the way an experienced agent reads a report. Sort the findings, lead with what matters, and never ask the seller to repair a toilet seat in the same email where you are asking them to credit a roof.
